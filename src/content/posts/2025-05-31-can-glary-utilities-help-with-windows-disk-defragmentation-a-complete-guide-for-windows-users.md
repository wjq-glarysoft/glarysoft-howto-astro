Fragmentation occurs when files are split and scattered across your hard drive, making it harder and slower for Windows to retrieve them. Understanding Disk Fragmentation and Its Impact

Fragmentation happens over time as you create, modify, and delete files on your hard drive. When files are broken into pieces and stored in various locations, the drive heads need to move around more to access them, leading to longer load times and increased wear and tear. Defragmentation reorganizes these fragments, placing them together so files can be accessed more quickly and efficiently.

Note: If your PC uses a solid-state drive (SSD), defragmentation is not necessary and, in fact, can reduce the lifespan of your drive. Instead, SSDs benefit from TRIM operations, which are managed automatically by Windows.

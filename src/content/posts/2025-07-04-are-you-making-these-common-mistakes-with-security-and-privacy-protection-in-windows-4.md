Windows is a powerful and versatile operating system, but its popularity also makes it a prime target for hackers, data thieves, and prying eyes. Whether you’re a beginner just getting started or an experienced user who wants to tighten up privacy and security, it’s easy to overlook critical settings and habits. Are you making these common mistakes with your Windows security and privacy protection? Read on to find out—and learn how to fix them with practical, step-by-step advice.

Why Security and Privacy Matter on Windows

With so many sensitive files, photos, and personal information stored on your PC, protecting your Windows system is essential. Security lapses can lead to malware infections, identity theft, or embarrassing data leaks. Meanwhile, privacy slip-ups can expose your browsing history, communications, and more to companies and even cybercriminals.

Beginner’s Section: Most Common Windows Security and Privacy Mistakes

Mistake 1: Using Weak or Reused Passwords

Many people still rely on easy-to-guess passwords or use the same password across multiple accounts. This makes it easy for cybercriminals to break in.

What to do: - Create complex passwords with a mix of letters, numbers, and symbols. - Use Windows Hello (facial recognition or fingerprint) where available for added security. - Consider a reputable password manager to generate and store strong passwords.

Mistake 2: Ignoring Windows Updates

System updates often include critical security patches. Skipping them leaves your PC vulnerable to new exploits.

What to do: - Open Settings > Update & Security > Windows Update. - Click “Check for updates” regularly or set updates to install automatically. - Restart your PC when updates require it.

Mistake 3: Overlooking Antivirus and Firewall Protection

Some users turn off Windows Defender or forget to enable their firewall, leaving the system open to threats.

What to do: - Ensure Windows Security (Defender) is turned on via Settings > Update & Security > Windows Security. - Check that the firewall is enabled through Windows Security > Firewall & network protection. - Consider a reputable third-party antivirus if you need advanced features.

Mistake 4: Not Managing App Permissions

Many apps request unnecessary access to your files, camera, or microphone.

What to do: - Go to Settings > Privacy. - Review permissions for Location, Camera, Microphone, etc. - Toggle off permissions for apps that don’t need them.

Mistake 5: Forgetting to Clear Privacy Data

Browsing history, cached files, and temporary data can accumulate and expose your activities or private information.

What to do: - Use your browser’s privacy or incognito mode for sensitive browsing. - Regularly clear browser history and cookies. - For a thorough cleanup, use Glary Utilities (see below for more details).

Advanced User Section: Tightening Up Windows Security and Privacy

Mistake 6: Failing to Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Relying on passwords alone isn’t enough for sensitive accounts.

What to do: - Enable 2FA on Microsoft accounts and important services. - Use an authenticator app or receive codes by SMS for an extra layer of protection.

Mistake 7: Not Encrypting Sensitive Files

If your device is lost or stolen, unencrypted files can be accessed easily.

What to do: - Use BitLocker (available in some Windows editions) to encrypt drives: Control Panel > System and Security > BitLocker Drive Encryption. - For individual files or folders, use built-in Windows encryption or third-party tools.

Mistake 8: Allowing Too Much Telemetry or Data Collection

Windows collects diagnostic data, but you can control how much you share.

What to do: - Go to Settings > Privacy > Diagnostics & feedback. - Choose “Required diagnostic data” instead of “Optional.” - Disable tailored ads and limit app access to your advertising ID.

Final Tips for All Users

\- Always lock your computer when away (Windows key + L). - Don’t install apps from untrusted sources. - Back up important data in case of ransomware or hardware failure. - Review privacy settings after major Windows updates, as they may reset.

Windows 11 offers a robust set of built-in power management tools that can dramatically impact your PC’s performance, longevity, and energy consumption. Why is Power Management Important in Windows 11?

Power management isn’t just about saving electricity. Optimized power settings can extend your device’s battery life, reduce heat and hardware strain, and even enhance system responsiveness. For desktops and laptops alike, avoiding common pitfalls is essential for a balanced computing experience.

Common Power Management Mistakes (and How to Fix Them)

Mistake 1: Sticking With Default Power Plans

Windows 11 comes with default power plans like Balanced, Power Saver, and High Performance. Many users never adjust these settings, leaving their PCs less efficient or unnecessarily power-hungry.

How to Fix:

1\. Open Settings. 2. Go to System > Power & Battery. 3. Under Power Mode, select the mode that best fits your needs, or click ‘Additional power settings’ to access classic options. 4. For custom control, select ‘Create a power plan’ and tailor sleep, display, and processor settings.

Pro tip: If you use demanding apps occasionally, create separate plans for daily work and high-performance tasks.

Mistake 2: Not Using Advanced Power Settings

Beginners often overlook advanced power settings, missing out on features like USB selective suspend, processor state management, and display sleep fine-tuning.

How to Fix:

1\. In Power Options (from Control Panel or Settings), click ‘Change plan settings’ beside your active plan. 2. Click ‘Change advanced power settings.’ 3. Review sections like Hard disk, Sleep, USB settings, Processor power management, and Graphics settings. 4. Adjust each to your usage—e.g., set ‘Turn off hard disk after’ to a reasonable time for SSD longevity, or tweak minimum processor state for better battery life.

Mistake 3: Ignoring Battery Saver and System Tools

Laptop users may forget to enable Battery Saver, or overlook powerful utilities that monitor and tweak battery health.

How to Fix:

1\. Open Settings > System > Power & battery. 2. Toggle Battery Saver on, or automate it to start at a certain battery percentage. 3. Use Glary Utilities’ Battery Monitor (found under Advanced Tools) to track battery wear, cycles, and performance. This helps spot issues early and optimize charging habits.

Mistake 4: Allowing Background Apps to Drain Power

Many background processes and startup programs sap power and resources, especially on laptops.

How to Fix:

1\. Open Settings > Apps > Startup. 2. Disable non-essential apps from launching at startup. 3. In Task Manager, review resource usage under the ‘Startup’ and ‘Processes’ tabs. 4. Use Glary Utilities’ Startup Manager to review, disable, or delay unnecessary apps and services for better power efficiency.

Mistake 5: Overusing Sleep or Never Using Hibernate

Some users put their PC to sleep constantly, never shutting down or hibernating, which can cause gradual memory leaks and system slowdowns.

How to Fix:

1\. In Power Options > Advanced settings, configure sleep and hibernate timers. 2. Use ‘Hibernate’ instead of ‘Sleep’ if you won’t use your PC for an extended period, especially on laptops. 3. Occasionally perform a full shutdown or restart to clear memory and maintain smooth operation.

Mistake 6: Not Updating Device Drivers

Outdated device drivers, especially for graphics and chipset, can interfere with modern power management features.

How to Fix:

1\. Open Device Manager (right-click Start). 2. Right-click on your critical devices (Display adapters, System devices, etc.), select ‘Update driver.’ 3. Use Windows Update to check for the latest drivers. 4. [Glary Utilities](https://www.glarysoft.com) offers a Driver Manager tool to help automate driver checks and updates, ensuring your power management features work as intended.

Mistake 7: Overlooking System Diagnostics

If your PC doesn't behave as expected (e.g., battery drains too fast, sleep doesn’t work), many users don’t run diagnostics to find the root cause.

How to Fix:

1\. Run Power Troubleshooter: Settings > System > Troubleshoot > Other troubleshooters > Power. 2. Use Windows’ built-in ‘powercfg /energy’ command in Command Prompt (as administrator) to generate a detailed report on power issues. 3. Analyze findings and apply suggested fixes.

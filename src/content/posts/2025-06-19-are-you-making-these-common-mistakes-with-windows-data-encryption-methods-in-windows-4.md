What Is Data Encryption in Windows, and Why Should You Care?

Encryption is the process of converting your data into a coded format so only authorized users can access it. On Windows, encryption options include BitLocker, Encrypted File System (EFS), and third-party solutions. Proper encryption protects sensitive files from unauthorized access, especially if your device is lost or stolen.

Beginner Mistakes: Avoid These to Save Time and Stay Secure

Not Enabling Built-in Encryption

Many Windows users assume their files are safe simply because they use a password to log in. However, without activating Windows’ encryption features, your data can still be easily accessed if your drive is removed and connected to another device.

Time-saving tip: Use BitLocker for full-disk encryption (available on Windows Pro and Enterprise editions). It’s straightforward to set up: 1. Open Control Panel and search for BitLocker. 2. Click "Turn on BitLocker" for your system drive. 3. Follow the on-screen prompts to set a strong PIN or password and save your recovery key in a safe location.

Relying Only on Password Protection

Password-protecting documents or folders isn’t the same as encrypting them. Many file types with password protection can be bypassed with free tools found online, leaving your data vulnerable.

Quick fix: Use the Encrypting File System (EFS) in Windows (also Pro and Enterprise editions) for individual files and folders: 1. Right-click the file or folder, select Properties, then click Advanced. 2. Check “Encrypt contents to secure data” and apply the changes.

For home users on Windows Home Edition, consider reputable third-party encryption utilities, but always research their credibility and support.

Losing Track of Recovery Keys

One of the most frustrating, time-consuming mistakes is misplacing your BitLocker or EFS recovery keys. Without them, you might permanently lose access to your data if you forget your password or change hardware.

Time-saving solution: - Store your recovery key in multiple secure locations: print a copy, save it to a USB drive, or use a password manager. - Consider using Glary Utilities’ Password Manager feature to securely store your encryption and recovery keys, ensuring you can always access your encrypted data quickly.

Advanced User Mistakes: Streamline Your Encryption Practices

Forgetting to Regularly Update Encryption Standards

Advanced users often set up BitLocker or EFS and then forget about them. Encryption algorithms and best practices evolve, and sticking with outdated methods can put your data at risk or slow down system performance.

Time-saving tip: Schedule a periodic review (e.g., every 6–12 months) to check for updates to Windows’ security features. When Windows introduces new encryption policies or improvements in algorithms, upgrading promptly can save you trouble in the long run.

Not Backing Up Encrypted Data Properly

Simply backing up encrypted files isn’t enough—you need to ensure that your backups can be restored and decrypted when needed. Copying encrypted files to another location without proper keys or context can result in inaccessible backups.

Best practice: - When backing up BitLocker-encrypted drives, use Windows Backup or a third-party tool that supports encrypted volumes. - Always back up your encryption keys alongside your data, using encrypted containers or password managers for storage. - Periodically test your backups to ensure you can restore and decrypt files without issues.

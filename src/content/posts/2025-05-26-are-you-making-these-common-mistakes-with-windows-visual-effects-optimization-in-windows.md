Many Windows users want to get the best possible performance from their systems, but visual effects settings are often overlooked or misunderstood. Windows visual effects include animations, shadows, and other graphical enhancements that can make your desktop look appealing but may impact system performance, especially on older or less powerful PCs. Are you making these common mistakes when optimizing visual effects? Let’s look at best practices, practical steps, and how tools like Glary Utilities can help.

Are You Leaving Unnecessary Visual Effects Enabled?

By default, Windows enables a range of visual effects designed to make the interface look modern and smooth. However, not all of these are necessary for daily tasks. If your PC feels sluggish, the first step is to review which effects are enabled.

How to Adjust Visual Effects Settings

1\. Right-click on the Start button and select System. 2. Click on “Advanced system settings” on the right sidebar. 3. In the Performance section, click Settings. 4. Under Visual Effects, choose “Adjust for best performance” to disable all effects, or select “Custom” to manually toggle each effect.

Practical Example: Disabling unneeded effects like “Animate windows when minimizing and maximizing” or “Fade or slide menus into view” can provide a noticeable speed boost, especially on PCs with limited RAM or older graphics hardware.

Are You Sacrificing Usability for Performance?

Some users go to the opposite extreme—turning off all visual effects without considering usability. While this can maximize speed, it may make the user interface harder to navigate or less visually distinct.

Best Practice: Strike a balance. Keep essential effects like “Smooth edges of screen fonts” for readability and “Show thumbnails instead of icons” for easier file previewing in Explorer.

Are You Forgetting to Revisit Settings After Upgrades?

Upgrading your RAM, graphics card, or moving to a newer version of Windows can greatly improve your system’s ability to handle visual effects. If you previously disabled effects to improve performance, you might be missing out on a better visual experience now.

Practical Tip: After a hardware or major system upgrade, revisit your visual effects settings and experiment with re-enabling certain options. Test the impact on performance and usability.
